**Relevance Score:** 6.5/10

The study investigates the prevalence of obscured active galactic nuclei (AGN) at high redshifts using the James Webb Space Telescope's (JWST) Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI). The researchers utilized deep MIRI imaging from the MEOW survey, combined with NIRCam, spectroscopic, and Hubble Space Telescope data, to identify a previously unrecognized population of obscured AGN up to z ~ 6. Through spectral energy distribution modeling, they identified 883 AGN over an area of ~131 arcmin² and constructed the AGN bolometric luminosity function across five redshift bins. The key finding is that the fraction of obscured AGN increases with redshift, reaching ~98-99% in the highest-redshift bin (4.5 < z < 6), indicating a substantial population missed by optical/UV surveys. This suggests that a significant portion of supermassive black hole growth at early cosmic times occurs during heavily obscured phases, largely inaccessible at other wavelengths.
